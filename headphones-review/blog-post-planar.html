The Planar Magnetic Revolution Nobody Asked For

Everyone's going planar. But are they actually better, or just different? A deep dive into magnets and marketing.

Remember when headphones were simple? You had drivers. They moved. Sound happened. Now everyone's obsessed with planars, and I'm starting to think we've collectively lost the plot.

Don't get me wrong—I own three pairs of planar magnetics. I've spent more time than I care to admit comparing the HiFiMAN Sundara to the Audeze LCD-2. But after years of listening, I've come to a controversial conclusion: most people buying planars would be happier with good dynamics.

The Planar Promise

Here's what planar evangelists will tell you: better transient response, lower distortion, more even frequency distribution across the driver. All technically true. The thin film diaphragm suspended between magnets does move more uniformly than a traditional cone driver. Physics doesn't lie.

But here's what they won't tell you: your ears probably can't tell the difference.

"The best headphone is the one that makes you forget you're wearing headphones. Most planars make you acutely aware you've strapped two waffle irons to your head."

The Weight Problem Nobody Talks About

My LCD-X weighs 635 grams. That's 1.4 pounds. Of magnets. On your head. For reference, the Sennheiser HD600—a headphone that sounds phenomenal and has been a studio standard for decades—weighs 260 grams.

You know what ruins musical immersion faster than 0.1% THD? Neck pain.

The Efficiency Elephant

Planars are power hungry. My phone can barely drive my Sundara to listenable levels. You need an amp. Then you need a better amp because you read on Head-Fi that your amp is holding back your headphones. Then you need a balanced amp because someone on Reddit said single-ended is leaving performance on the table.

Meanwhile, my Grado SR80x sounds fantastic out of anything with a headphone jack.

But What About The Sound?

Yes, good planars sound incredible. The Audeze LCD-X remains one of the most technically impressive headphones I've heard. The bass extension is real. The midrange clarity is addictive. But you know what else sounds incredible? The Focal Clear. The ZMF Auteur. The humble Sennheiser HD650.

All dynamics. All easier to drive. All more comfortable for long listening sessions.

The Real Revolution

The planar revolution isn't about sound quality—it's about having something new to sell. The headphone market was getting stale. Everyone already owned HD600s or DT990s. Enter planars: a "new" technology (that's actually from the 1970s) that gives reviewers something to write about and consumers a reason to upgrade.

Am I saying planars are bad? No. Some of my favorite headphones are planars. But the idea that they're categorically superior to dynamics is marketing nonsense that's convinced too many people to buy headphones they don't actually enjoy using.

The Bottom Line

If you're considering planars, ask yourself: Do I want the best measuring headphone, or do I want to enjoy music? Because those aren't always the same thing.

The revolution already happened. It was called "making headphones that sound good and are comfortable to wear." Everything else is just expensive noise.

— Dakota Chen
