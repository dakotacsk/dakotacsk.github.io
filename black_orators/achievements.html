FIVE ORATORS AND THEIR ACHIEVEMENTS

Background track: The Staple Singers - Respect Yourself

Soujourner Truth, Maya Angelou, Marcus Garvey, Audre Lorde, and Ross Gay are five Black orators from different periods. Soujourner Truth, an abolitionist and women's rights activist, paved the way for African American women to have a voice in society. Maya Angelou, an acclaimed poet, and civil rights activist, used her writing to shed light on issues of race and gender. Marcus Garvey, a Jamaican-born activist, advocated for the empowerment of black people and the establishment of a separate black nation. Audre Lorde, a writer, and feminist, fought for social justice and equality for all marginalized communities. Ross Gay, a contemporary poet, focuses on love, joy, and community themes in his work. Despite the differences in their specific focuses, these speakers share a common thread of delivering speeches that address the needs and aspirations of the black community. Their heartfelt manner of delivery, combined with the sentiments they convey, creates a powerful legacy of activism, empowerment, resilience, and healing that continues to resonate within the black community today.
Selected Ross Gay speeches.


1797
Birth of Soujourner Truth


Sojourner Truth was an inspiring African American abolitionist and women's rights activist. Truth was a formerly enslaved person who advocated abolition, temperance, and civil and women's rights in the 19th century. She was born Isabella Bomfree in New York and was sold four times. In 1827, she escaped to a nearby abolitionist family who helped her gain her freedom and sue for the return of her son. In the early 1830s, Truth met abolitionists William Lloyd Garrison and Frederick Douglass and became a powerful speaker and traveled throughout the country, advocating for the abolition of slavery and women's suffrage.

1851
Soujourner Truth's "Ain't I a Woman"

Her famous speech, "Ain't I a Woman?" delivered at the Women's Rights Convention in 1851, highlighted the intersectionality of gender and race in the fight for equality. Truth's legacy continues to inspire and empower people to this day.

1883
Passing Away of Soujourner Truth

Truth settled in Michigan during the 1850s and helped enslaved people escape to freedom. During the Civil War, she organized supplies for Black troops and worked with the Freedmen's Bureau to help formerly enslaved people find jobs. She was invited to meet President Abraham Lincoln in 1864. She spent her final years in Michigan and advocated for formerly enslaved people to receive land.

1887
Birth of Marcus Garvey


Garvey was a powerful speaker and orator who inspired many during his time. He had a way with words that captivated his audiences and empowered them. Garvey believed in the importance of self-reliance and unity among Black people, and his speeches reflected these values. His message of pride and self-determination resonated with many and continues to inspire people today.

1921
Marcus Garvey's "Address to the Second UNIA Convention"

In 1921, Garvey delivered a speech at the second UNIA convention in New York as the founder of the association. He emphasized the UNIA's mission to emancipate black people worldwide and redeem Africa. Garvey criticized the League of Nations for dividing African territories without consulting the African people. He also condemned racial prejudice, segregation, and injustice faced by black people, warning that such actions would lead to the downfall of civilization. Garvey foresaw a future conflict between races and urged white, black, and yellow individuals with a vision for peace and human brotherhood to spread the message of love and unity. He believed that Africa had untapped potential and called for a change in the white world's attitude towards weaker races. Garvey stressed the need for compromise and fairness in the global order, allowing black people the opportunity to govern themselves and demonstrate their capabilities.

1928
Birth of Maya Angelou

Maya Angelou was a remarkable poet, memoirist, and civil rights activist. She used her powerful words to inspire and empower people worldwide, especially women and people of color. Her autobiographical works, including "I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings," shed light on the struggles and triumphs of her life and those of others who faced discrimination and prejudice. Angelou's legacy inspires generations to speak up for justice and equality and find strength in their stories.

1934
Birth of Audre Lorde

Audre Lorde was an influential poet and writer who used her words to inspire and empower marginalized communities. Her work focused on race, gender, sexuality, and identity, and she was known for her unapologetic approach to these topics. Lorde's legacy is of fearlessness and strength, and her words resonate with readers today. She is truly a trailblazer and an inspiration to us all. Some of her most important works include "The Transformation of Silence into Language and Action," "Sister Outsider," and "Coal." These works speak to the struggles of marginalized communities and the importance of speaking out against oppression. Lorde's words remind us that we are all responsible for using our voices to create positive change in the world.

1940
Passing Away of Marcus Garvey

After suffering from multiple strokes, he passed away on June 10, 1940. Despite his contentious reputation, Garvey's lasting impact continued to foster pride and inspiration among countless individuals of African descent across the globe. His beliefs have shaped the thinking of those involved in the Nation of Islam, the Black Power movement, and the Rastafari movement.

1970
Audre Lorde's "In Cables to Rage"

"In Cables to Rage", Audre Lorde fearlessly harnessed her fury, confronting societal and individual inequities head-on. This collection not only served as a powerful outlet for her emotions but also marked a significant milestone as she fearlessly explored her lesbian identity through the medium of poetry.

1974
Birth of Ross Gay

Ross Gay is a phenomenal poet and essayist known for his powerful and insightful works that explore themes of love, joy, and the complexities of identity. His writing is deeply personal and often touches on issues of race, gender, and sexuality, as well as the beauty and wonder of the natural world. With a unique voice that is both candid and compassionate, Ross Gay has become a beloved figure in the literary community, inspiring readers and fellow writers alike with his wisdom, wit, and profound understanding of the human experience.

1976
Audre Lorde's "With Coal"

Through "With Coal," Lorde made her remarkable entrance into the literary world, earning widespread praise for this compilation of her earlier works. As her major publisher debut, Coal showcased Lorde's exceptional talent and garnered recognition for her unflinching exploration of social and personal themes with poetic finesse.

1978
Audre Lorde's "The Black Unicorn"

This piece represents a defining moment in Audre Lorde's poetic odyssey. Departing from urban themes, Lorde immerses herself in Africa, unveiling a profound exploration of her multifaceted identities as a mother and daughter. Through evocative imagery and a rich tapestry of mythology, she crafts a masterpiece that resonates with depth and authenticity.

1993
Maya Angelou's "On the Pulse of Morning"

Maya Angelou's "On the Pulse of Morning" is a poignant and evocative poem delivered at the inauguration of President Bill Clinton in 1993. This powerful piece embodies the resilience and spirit of the African American experience, urging listeners to embrace their shared humanity and rise above ignorance and division. Angelou's delivery of "On the Pulse of Morning" was marked by her commanding presence and skillful use of pauses and intonation, which added depth and power to the poem. Her deliberate pacing allowed the words to resonate and created dramatic tension, capturing the attention of the audience. Angelou's voice rose and fell with emotion, emphasizing key phrases and invoking a sense of urgency. Through her dynamic delivery, she conveyed the weight of history, the call for action, and the hope for a better future. Angelou's masterful use of power and pauses made her poem even more impactful, leaving a lasting impression on all who heard it.

1994
Maya Angelou's "Phenomenal Woman"

This speech was a tribute to women, celebrating their strength, resilience, and beauty. It spoke to the idea that women can achieve anything they set their minds to and should be proud of who they are.

1995
Maya Angelou's "A Brave and Startling Truth"

Maya Angelou's "A Brave and Startling Truth" is a profound poem that delivers a powerful message about the interconnectedness of humanity. Originally delivered in June 1995 during the 50th anniversary commemoration of the United Nations, this poem marked another milestone in Angelou's career as a distinguished poet and speaker. Through her eloquent delivery and poignant words, Angelou inspires reflection, unity, and the recognition of truth, leaving an indelible mark on black oratorical history.

2014
Maya Angelou's "A Brave and Startling Truth"

"A Brave and Startling Truth" (1995) - This speech was delivered at the 50th anniversary of the United Nations in 1995. It addressed the issues of war, poverty, and inequality and called for a world where all people could live in peace and harmony.

2015
Ross Gay's "Catalog of Unabashed Gratitude"

"Catalog of Unabashed Gratitude" is one of Ross Gay's most celebrated poetry collections. It is the recipient of the Kingsley Tufts Award and captures moments of profound gratitude in the face of life's complexities. It was a finalist for prestigious literary honors, including the National Book Award and the National Book Critics Circle Award.

2019
Ross Gay's "Catalog of Unabashed Gratitude"

In this essay collection, Ross Gay shares delightful observations from his daily life, illuminating the beauty found in ordinary moments. With his lyrical prose and keen insights, he invites readers to appreciate the small joys that often go unnoticed.

2022
Ross Gay's "Inciting Joy: Essays"

"Inciting Joy: Essays" continues Gay's exploration of joy and healing, Ross Gay delves into his relationship with his father and examines the transformative power of joy within the Black community. This essay collection offers introspection, resilience, and a pathway to collective healing and liberation.
